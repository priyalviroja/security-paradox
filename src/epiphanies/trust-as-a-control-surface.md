<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Story</span>

A company implemented zero-trust architecture. Every request required authentication. Every action required authorization. Every access was logged and monitored.

Productivity plummeted. Engineers spent hours getting approvals for routine tasks. Deployments that took minutes now took days. Innovation slowed to a crawl.

Six months later, engineers had found workarounds for every control. They shared credentials, cached tokens, and built shadow systems. The zero-trust architecture was still there—on paper. In practice, it was Swiss cheese.

They had eliminated trust from the system. So trust found another way.

The question isn't whether to trust. It's *where* to place your trust—and what happens when you get it wrong.
</div>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Reflection</span>

Trust is not the opposite of security. Trust *is* a security control.

Every system has trust boundaries—implicit or explicit. The question is whether you've designed them intentionally or whether they've emerged organically (and dangerously).

Zero-trust doesn't mean "trust nothing." It means "trust explicitly, verify continuously, and minimize the blast radius when trust is violated."

The paradox: systems with no trust are impossible to use. Systems with too much trust are impossible to secure.

The art is finding the balance:
- Trust people, but verify their actions
- Trust systems, but monitor their behavior
- Trust processes, but audit their outcomes

And when trust is violated—because it will be—ensure the damage is contained.

Trust is not a binary. It's a spectrum, a surface, a design choice.
</div>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Principle</span>

<div class="principle-takeaway">
"Trust is a control surface, not a vulnerability. Design trust boundaries intentionally. The goal is not zero trust—it's right-sized, explicit, verifiable trust."
</div>
</div>
