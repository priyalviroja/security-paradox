<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Story</span>

A startup founder once told me: "We move fast and break things. Security slows us down."

I asked: "How many times have you rebuilt the authentication system?"

"Three times," he admitted. "We keep finding issues."

"And how long did each rebuild take?"

"About two months each time."

I smiled. "So you've spent six months moving fast in the wrong direction. That's speed without velocity."

Speed is motion. Velocity is motion *in the right direction*. You can run very fast in circles and never arrive anywhere.
</div>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Reflection</span>

Security leaders often face the "speed vs. security" false dichotomy. But this framing misses the point entirely.

The real question isn't "How do we balance speed and security?" It's "How do we achieve velocity—sustainable, directional progress—instead of just speed?"

Technical debt is speed without velocity. Shipping features with vulnerabilities is speed without velocity. Moving fast while accumulating risk is speed without velocity.

True velocity requires:
- Direction (knowing where you're going)
- Sustainability (not burning out or breaking down)
- Learning (getting better with each iteration)

Security, done well, *increases* velocity. It reduces rework, prevents costly breaches, builds trust with customers, and creates sustainable momentum.

The teams that move fastest in the long run are the ones who build security in from the start.
</div>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Principle</span>

<div class="principle-takeaway">
"Optimize for velocity, not speed. Security is not a brake—it's a compass. It ensures you're moving fast in the right direction."
</div>
