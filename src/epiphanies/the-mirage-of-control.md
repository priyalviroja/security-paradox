<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Story</span>

A CISO once showed me a dashboard with 47 security controls, all green. "We're fully protected," they said with confidence. Three weeks later, a developer's personal laptop—outside every control boundary—became the entry point for a breach that cost millions.

Every leader believes they can control risk. We build walls, deploy sensors, write policies. We create the illusion of a perimeter in a world that has none. Control is a story we tell ourselves to stay sane in the face of infinite complexity.

But control is a mirage. The tighter you grip, the more slips through your fingers.
</div>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Reflection</span>

What if security leadership isn't about control at all?

Mature leaders measure *influence*, not control. They shape behavior, culture, and incentives. They design systems that assume breach, that expect failure, that plan for chaos.

They ask: "When this fails—not if—how quickly can we detect it? How gracefully can we degrade? How fast can we recover?"

Control is finite. Influence is infinite.
</div>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<div class="epiphany-section">
<span class="epiphany-label">The Principle</span>

<div class="principle-takeaway">
"Graceful degradation — design systems that fail well and recover quickly. Security is not the absence of failure; it's the elegance of recovery."
</div>
</div>
